Context

What's actually happening here?

You've probably studied market failure — situations where individually rational behaviour produces outcomes that are bad for everyone. Classic examples: pollution, overfishing, congested roads.

What's happening in this WhatsApp thread is related. But it isn't a market failure, exactly.

It's more specific than that. There's a structural logic to why 163 people read that message and most of them will do nothing. It was documented by an economist in 1965 — and it holds up remarkably well as a description of how students, unions, political parties, and entire governments behave.

First, though — let's see what happens when 1pm arrives.

The Concept

You already knew this.

When you said earlier that you've held back from joining something while waiting to see if others would — you were reasoning exactly the way an economist would predict.

In 1965, a political economist named Mancur Olson published a book that unsettled conventional thinking about how groups behave. His central argument was simple, counterintuitive, and uncomfortable:

Most of the thread was reasoning in a particular way.

The logic holds even if you personally wouldn't have hesitated. Most of the students on that thread were making a specific calculation — one that an economist named Mancur Olson described in 1965 and that has never really been refuted since.

His central argument was simple, counterintuitive, and uncomfortable:

Rational people who share a common interest will not organise to pursue it — unless something forces them to.

This is the free-rider problem.

Here's the logic, applied to Ajay's petition:

  • Clean air is a non-excludable benefit. If the campaign succeeds, everyone benefits — whether they signed or not.
  • Which means: if enough other people do the work, you benefit without contributing anything.
  • And your individual contribution — one signature, two hours at a meeting — is unlikely to be the one that tips the outcome.
  • So the individually rational calculation is: wait and see if others do it.

When 200 people make this calculation simultaneously, nobody goes. That is not apathy. That is not ignorance. That is rational self-interest, operating exactly as Olson predicted.

"Unless the number of individuals in a group is quite small, or unless there is coercion or some other special device to make individuals act in their common interest, rational, self-interested individuals will not act to achieve their common or group interests." — Mancur Olson, The Logic of Collective Action (1965)

The Concept (continued)

Group size, and the solution.

Olson didn't just diagnose the problem. He identified what structural conditions make collective action possible — and what makes it likely to fail.

Small groups can often self-organise. When there are four flatmates splitting a shared electricity bill, each person's share of the cost is substantial and visible. Free-riding is hard to hide.

But try organising 800 million mobile phone users to campaign against high data prices. Each user's potential benefit from a successful campaign is tiny relative to the cost of participating. Nobody's individual contribution matters. Everyone free-rides. The campaign doesn't happen.

Olson called large groups that can't self-organise latent groups. They have shared interests but no mechanism to act on them.

The newspaper offer in Branch B worked because it introduced a selective incentive — a benefit that goes only to people who participate. Visibility. Name in print. Peer recognition.

This breaks the free-rider logic. Now, even if the campaign fails, participants receive something non-participants don't. The calculation changes.

Olson's argument: without selective incentives (or coercion), large groups will not organise around shared interests, regardless of how much those interests matter.

1. Selective incentives (positive): Private benefits available only to members or contributors. Trade union members get legal support, insurance, discounted services. Non-members get nothing.

2. Coercion (negative): Cost applied to non-participants. The closed shop — where union membership is a condition of employment — eliminates the free-rider option. You either join or you don't work.

3. Institutional design: Reduce group size. Increase visibility of individual contributions. Make defection costly through social pressure or monitoring. The hardest to engineer at scale, but the most durable when it works.

What Olson explains here

This is the latent group problem. A group of 3,000 students is too large to self-organise around a non-excludable benefit without an external mechanism. Each individual faces a rational incentive to defect. Without a structural intervention, the equilibrium is non-participation — even when almost everyone agrees that cleaner air is desirable.

Wanting the outcome and contributing to it are two different rational calculations.

Branch B · Consequence + Framework

The payoff structure changed.

The canteen discount changes the calculation for most students within 24 hours.

Opting in is no longer an act of altruism — it's a financially rational decision. The benefit (₹200/month in canteen savings) is private, excludable, and immediate. The cost (leaving your vehicle at home one day a week) is manageable for most students with access to public transport.

Opt-in rate at the end of week one: 61%. The university spends approximately ₹12 per participating student per week — far cheaper than any enforcement mechanism it had considered.

What Olson explains here

The selective incentive worked not by appealing to environmental values, but by restructuring the payoff matrix. Participation became individually rational — the private benefit (discount) exceeded the private cost (inconvenience).

The university didn't change what was rational. It changed the structure so that the individually rational choice aligned with the collectively beneficial one.

Branch C · Consequence + Framework

One extra question was enough.

The counter-campaign doesn't need to be correct. It just needs to add a step.

Before the counter-message, students had one question: Do I opt in? After, they have two: Is this campaign credible? And do I opt in?

For the students who were already on the fence — which, in any large group facing a collective action problem, is most of them — this second question is decisive.

Opt-in rate after one week: 6%. The campaign trends on college social media — but for the wrong reasons.

What Olson explains here

Large groups attempting collective action exist in an unstable equilibrium. The default is non-participation; the momentum toward participation is fragile. A small increase in coordination cost — even from a minority actor, even without persuading anyone — is enough to tip the equilibrium back to the free-rider outcome.

Olson's logic cuts both ways: it explains why collective action is hard to build, and why it's easy to disrupt.

Application

Three cases from India.

Olson's framework extends well beyond student politics. Read each case and identify the mechanism at work — then reveal the answer.

Case 1 — MNREGA and the guaranteed wage

Under MNREGA, rural workers are entitled to 100 days of paid work annually. Attendance is recorded. Wages are paid only to those who show up and work. Non-participants receive nothing.

Despite the collective interest all rural workers share in a functional public employment programme, individual participation is high in districts where the scheme operates well.

What Olsonian mechanism is at work?

Answer

Coercion (negative selective incentive). The benefit — the wage — is fully excludable. Non-participation means no wage. There is no free-rider option. Participation is individually rational because the benefit is tied directly to individual contribution.

Case 2 — Punjab/Haryana farmers' unions

Large agricultural unions in Punjab and Haryana maintain substantial membership bases. Members receive access to subsidised seeds through union procurement networks, legal assistance in land disputes, and group crop insurance. Non-members receive none of these services.

Membership fees are modest. Retention is high.

What Olsonian mechanism is at work?

Answer

Selective incentives (positive). The union offers a bundle of private, excludable benefits — seeds, legal support, insurance — that make membership individually rational independent of any collective campaign. Even a member who disagrees with the union's political positions has a financial reason to stay. This is why strong unions build service infrastructure, not just shared identity.

Case 3 — Urban environmental NGOs

Environmental organisations in Indian cities consistently report difficulty sustaining mass membership, despite high public concern about air and water quality. Survey data shows widespread agreement that environmental action is necessary. Donation campaigns underperform relative to stated concern.

What Olsonian mechanism is at work — or rather, what's missing?

Answer

The absence of selective incentives. Clean air and clean water are textbook non-excludable benefits. Olson's model predicts exactly this pattern: widespread stated concern combined with chronic under-participation. Environmental NGOs that survive long-term typically offer expressive selective incentives — identity, community, social recognition — as private goods distributed to members only.
